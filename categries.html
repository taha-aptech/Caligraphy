Language Categories Explained

A group of languages with a common ancestral origin. Example: Arabic → Semitic branch of Afro-Asiatic family

Script Direction

Writing orientation: Arabic and Persian: Right to Left. Greek, Indic, Chinese (modern): Left to Right.

Arabic Language

Arabic is a Semitic language, part of the Afro-Asiatic family, and originated in the Arabian Peninsula. Earliest Evidence: The earliest Arabic inscriptions date back to the 4th century CE (Safaitic and Nabataean scripts). Qur’anic Arabic: With the revelation of the Quran in the 7th century CE, Classical Arabic became standardized. Expansion: After the rise of Islam, Arabic spread across North Africa, the Middle East, and parts of Europe (Spain). Influence: Arabic contributed to science, philosophy, and medicine in the Islamic Golden Age; it influenced many languages including Spanish, Persian, Urdu, and Turkish.

Greek Language

Origin: Greek is an Indo-European language and has been spoken in the Balkans since at least 2000 BCE. Ancient Greek: Divided into dialects (Doric, Ionic, Aeolic); Classical Greek flourished in the 5th-4th century BCE (Plato, Aristotle). Koine Greek: Became the common language of the Hellenistic world after Alexander the Great. Byzantine Greek: Used in the Eastern Roman Empire; evolved toward modern forms. Modern Greek: Spoken today in Greece and Cyprus; has retained much of its ancient structure and vocabulary.

Persian Language (Farsi)

Origin: Persian is also an Indo-European language from the Iranian branch. Old Persian: Used during the Achaemenid Empire (6th century BCE), seen in royal inscriptions. Middle Persian: Language of the Sassanid Empire (3rd–7th century CE), also called Pahlavi. Modern Persian: Emerged after the Islamic conquest of Persia. Written in Arabic script. Influence: Rich poetic tradition (e.g., Rumi, Hafez), and major impact on Urdu, Turkish, and Mughal India.

Chinese Language

Origin: Chinese is a Sino-Tibetan language, with roots going back over 3,000 years. Old Chinese: Used during the Zhou dynasty (c. 1046–256 BCE), found in inscriptions on bones and bronze. Classical Chinese: Language of literature and administration from the Spring and Autumn Period to the early 20th century. Mandarin Chinese: The modern standard, based on the Beijing dialect; uses logographic script (characters). Continuity: One of the world’s oldest living languages with a continuous written tradition.

Indic (Indian) Languages

Origin: Part of the Indo-Aryan branch of the Indo-European family; emerged in northern India. Vedic Sanskrit: Language of the Vedas (c. 1500 BCE); very ancient form of Sanskrit. Classical Sanskrit: Codified by Panini (~4th century BCE); used in religion, philosophy, and science. Prakrits & Pali: Evolved from Sanskrit; used in Buddhist and Jain texts. Modern Languages: Includes Hindi, Bengali, Punjabi, Marathi, Gujarati, Urdu, and many others. Script Diversity: Written in multiple scripts (e.g., Devanagari, Gurmukhi, Bengali).
